# Decision Framework

Every decision deserves the right amount of process — not too much, not too little. Use the layer that matches the stakes. Most decisions belong in Layer 1. A few need Layer 3.

---

## Layer 1: Quick Filter (30 seconds)

Run these three questions before spending more time on any decision:

**1. Is this reversible?**
If yes — decide fast and move on. Reversible decisions are cheap to make and cheap to fix. Over-deliberating them is waste.

**2. What's the cost of delay?**
If low — sleep on it. Good decisions made under time pressure are rarely better than good decisions made with rest.

**3. Am I the right person to decide this?**
If no — delegate immediately. Holding a decision you shouldn't own blocks both you and the right decision-maker.

If the decision passes all three filters (irreversible, time-sensitive, and yours to make), move to Layer 2.

---

## Layer 2: 10/10/10 Rule (2 minutes)

From Suzy Welch's framework — forces temporal perspective.

- **In 10 minutes**, how will I feel about this choice?
- **In 10 months**, looking back, will this have mattered the way it feels like it does now?
- **In 10 years**, what will I wish I had done?

The power: most decisions that feel urgent shrink significantly at the 10-month and 10-year horizon. When the 10-year answer is clear, that's usually your answer.

If the decision is still genuinely hard after this, move to Layer 3.

---

## Layer 3: Full Decision Template

For decisions with real stakes, real irreversibility, or real complexity. Budget 30-60 minutes.

### Step 1: Define

State the decision clearly in one sentence. If you can't do this, you don't understand the decision yet. Keep refining until you can.

> "Should I [specific action] given [specific context]?"

### Step 2: Gather

Answer these before evaluating options:

- What information do I actually need to decide well?
- What am I missing that could change my answer?
- Who has relevant experience with this decision?
- What's the fastest way to get the information that matters most?

Don't gather information indefinitely. Set a deadline.

### Step 3: Evaluate

Build an options table:

| Option | Pros | Cons | Reversible? | Confidence |
|--------|------|------|-------------|------------|
| Option A | | | Yes / No | High / Med / Low |
| Option B | | | Yes / No | High / Med / Low |
| Option C | | | Yes / No | High / Med / Low |

Notes on the table:
- **Reversible?** — if yes, this option deserves a lower bar for action
- **Confidence** — your confidence that the pros/cons are accurate, not just that you like the option
- Include a "do nothing" row if inaction is genuinely an option

### Step 4: Decide

Choose. Write down WHY in two to three sentences. The discipline of writing the rationale separate from the outcome is the whole game — from Annie Duke's *Thinking in Bets*, decisions should be evaluated on the quality of the reasoning at the time, not the outcome.

> **Decision:** [What you chose]
> **Rationale:** [Why this and not the alternatives — in your own words]

### Step 5: Commit

A decision without a next action is not a decision.

- **Next action:** [Specific first step]
- **Owner:** [Who does it]
- **Timeline:** [By when]
- **Review date:** [When to evaluate the outcome]

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Analysis paralysis** — More research rarely changes a decision you're already equipped to make. Set a decide-by date.

**Outcome bias** — A bad outcome doesn't mean a bad decision. A good outcome doesn't mean a good decision. Judge the reasoning, not the result.

**FOMO options** — Adding more options to feel thorough often makes decisions worse. Three strong options beats ten weak ones.

**Consensus seeking** — Not every decision benefits from more input. Know when you need to decide alone.

**Sunk cost anchoring** — Past investment is irrelevant to future value. Evaluate from where you are, not where you started.
